How EMI Is Calculated

Standard EMI Formula: EMI = P × R × (1+R)^N / ((1+R)^N - 1), where P is the loan amount, R is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 100 ÷ 12), and N is the total number of months.

Example: For a ₹25 lakh home loan at 7.5% annual interest over 20 years: Monthly EMI ≈ ₹18,728. Over 240 months, you pay ₹44.94 lakhs total, meaning ₹19.94 lakhs is pure interest.

Prepayment Benefit: Extra payments reduce the principal faster, lowering the interest charged on remaining months. Reducing tenure saves maximum interest but keeps EMI high. Reducing EMI lowers your monthly burden but may extend the loan.

Frequently Asked Questions About EMI & Loans

What is the relationship between loan amount, tenure, and EMI?

Increasing tenure spreads the loan over more months, lowering monthly EMI but increasing total interest paid. Conversely, shorter tenure means higher EMI but lower total interest. Find the balance that fits your monthly budget.

How does interest rate affect my EMI?

Interest rate is a percentage of the loan principal charged yearly. A higher rate means higher EMI and more total interest paid. Even 0.5% difference in interest can significantly impact your long-term cost. Always compare rates across lenders.

Can prepayment reduce my EMI without extending the loan?

Yes! Use the "Keep Tenure Same, Reduce Monthly EMI" mode. Prepayment reduces your outstanding principal, and the lender recalculates EMI based on the lower balance, freeing up your monthly budget.

Is prepayment penalty charged by banks?

Most banks charge prepayment penalties only on floating-rate loans (in some cases) or loans transferred between lenders. Fixed-rate home loans generally allow penalty-free prepayment. Check your loan agreement or contact your bank.

What is the difference between EMI and total interest?

EMI is your fixed monthly payment. Total interest is the cumulative interest paid over the entire loan life. Total Payment = (EMI × Number of Months) = Principal + Total Interest.

What Is an EMI Calculator?

An EMI calculator helps you estimate the monthly installment you need to pay when taking a loan. EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment, which means you pay a fixed amount every month for the selected tenure. This amount includes both principal repayment and interest. For Indian borrowers, this is useful in many real-life situations: choosing between banks, checking affordability before applying, planning monthly budget, and understanding the total repayment burden over years. Whether you are evaluating a home loan, car loan, or personal loan, the EMI output gives an immediate reality check. Instead of guessing if a loan is "manageable," you can compare EMI with your monthly income and existing expenses. A good approach is to test multiple scenarios by changing tenure and interest rates, then choose a safer EMI range that keeps room for emergencies. When you add prepayment assumptions, the calculator also helps you understand how faster principal reduction can cut long-term interest cost.

How It Works (Formula in Simple Terms)

The EMI formula used by banks is: EMI = P × R × (1+R)^N / ((1+R)^N - 1). Here, P is the principal loan amount, R is monthly interest rate (annual rate divided by 12 and then by 100), and N is total number of months. The formula balances principal and interest across the tenure so your installment remains largely constant. In early months, a bigger part of EMI goes toward interest. Over time, the interest part decreases and principal repayment increases. That is why long tenures reduce EMI but increase total interest paid. This page also calculates total interest and total payment, so you can see the hidden long-term cost, not just the monthly EMI. Tips and Practical Use Cases

For home loans, compare at least three banks and test each interest rate in this calculator before finalizing. For car loans, check whether a shorter tenure saves enough interest to justify higher EMI. For personal loans, be more conservative because rates are often higher and prepayment planning becomes more important. Try this sequence: first calculate base EMI with no prepayment, then test monthly extra prepayment and one-time lump sum prepayment. If your goal is maximum interest savings, choose reducing tenure. If your goal is easier cash flow, choose reducing EMI. Also remember to keep a safety buffer in your monthly budget instead of selecting the highest possible EMI. In practice, many borrowers target a comfortable EMI-to-income ratio and keep emergency funds separate.
